# Audit Model

Use this reference to choose and define the 4+3 audit axes. The goal is not to mention every theorist, but to use the smallest set of concepts that explains the object precisely.

## 4 Main Axes

### 1. Physical-Neural Axis

Question: How does the object act on the body and nervous system before reflective thought begins?

Use for: cinema, short video, music, sound, interface design, games, spectacle, propaganda, architecture, commuting, fatigue, addiction, attention loops.

Core mechanism:
- External signals such as light, sound, vibration, rhythm, tempo, contrast, repetition, acceleration, and feedback can bypass deliberation and organize perception.
- The first audit target is not meaning but signal: what enters the eyes, ears, skin, posture, breath, pulse, sleep, and attention cycle.

Useful lenses:
- Walter Benjamin: shock effect; montage and technical reproduction interrupt contemplative perception.
- Friedrich Kittler: media materialism; hardware and technical systems shape what can be said, stored, heard, and thought.
- Uri Hasson / neurocinematics: audiovisual structure can synchronize attention and neural response across viewers.

Guardrail:
- Do not reduce the person to a machine as a factual claim. Treat "biochemical receiver" as an analytical compression, not a complete anthropology.

### 2. Social Discipline Axis

Question: How does the object train, classify, normalize, rank, or produce subjects?

Use for: institutions, schools, workplaces, platforms, cities, commuting, family roles, credentials, class, taste, surveillance, self-management, moral obligations.

Core mechanism:
- Power often works by producing normal behavior instead of merely prohibiting behavior.
- Institutions create legible bodies, schedules, identities, credentials, desires, and self-monitoring habits.

Useful lenses:
- Michel Foucault: discipline, surveillance, normalization, the internalized gaze.
- Louis Althusser: interpellation; ideology calls individuals into subject positions.
- Pierre Bourdieu: field, habitus, cultural capital, distinction, symbolic violence.

Guardrail:
- Do not label every social pattern as oppression. Identify the actual mechanism of selection, reward, punishment, classification, or internalization.

### 3. Ontological Existence Axis

Question: What does the object reveal about being, finitude, authenticity, becoming, reality, or the loss of reality?

Use for: philosophy, life choices, death, boredom, routine, digital identity, alienation, existential crisis, art that changes one's sense of reality.

Core mechanism:
- Some objects do not merely communicate content; they disclose a mode of existence.
- Audit whether the object awakens, anesthetizes, fragments, simulates, or transforms one's relation to being.

Useful lenses:
- Martin Heidegger: everydayness, they-self, being-towards-death, authenticity.
- Gilles Deleuze: becoming, difference, lines of flight, deterritorialization.
- Jean Baudrillard: simulacra, hyperreality, replacement of the real by signs.

Guardrail:
- Do not inflate ordinary preference into ontology. Use this axis only when the object changes the structure of meaning, time, selfhood, reality, or possibility.

### 4. Desire-Mirror Axis

Question: What desire does the object organize, reflect, displace, or exploit?

Use for: ambition, shame, identity, fandom, consumer desire, romance, self-improvement, intellectual hunger, status anxiety, addiction, fantasy, resentment.

Core mechanism:
- Desire is rarely transparent. The apparent object may be a screen for lack, fantasy, recognition, aggression, sublimation, or status repair.
- Audit the gap between what the subject says they want and what the structure of pursuit reveals.

Useful lenses:
- Jacques Lacan: mirror stage, lack, ideal ego, symbolic order.
- Slavoj Zizek: fantasy, ideology, objet petit a, surplus enjoyment.
- Sigmund Freud: libido, repression, displacement, sublimation.

Guardrail:
- Psychoanalytic readings are high-risk. Present them as interpretations, not diagnoses, unless there is direct evidence.

## 3 Secondary Axes

### 5. Narrative-Logic Axis

Question: What causal chain, plot grammar, or sense-making template is organizing the object?

Use for: film, novels, personal life narratives, startup stories, social media arcs, propaganda, biography, self-improvement myths, historical explanation.

Core mechanism:
- Narrative imposes necessity on contingency. It can clarify causality, but it can also hide randomness, violence, institutional conditions, and unresolved contradiction.

Useful lenses:
- Aristotle: plot completeness, necessity, beginning-middle-end, causal intelligibility.
- Vladimir Propp: recurring story functions and standardized narrative parts.
- Robert McKee: engineered emotional turns, conflict, crisis, reversal, industrial storytelling.

Guardrail:
- Do not treat every coherent narrative as manipulation. Ask whether the causal chain reveals reality or merely pacifies the audience.

### 6. Aesthetics-Rhetoric Axis

Question: How do beauty, taste, style, metaphor, composition, and rhetoric make something acceptable, desirable, or authoritative?

Use for: visual art, design, literature, music, brand language, political rhetoric, architecture, lifestyle imagery, "high taste", "sincerity", "craft", "naturalness".

Core mechanism:
- Aesthetic form can reveal truth, but it can also cover power, class distinction, commercial standardization, and ideological framing.
- Rhetoric converts one meaning into another and often smuggles judgment through style.

Useful lenses:
- Immanuel Kant: purposiveness without purpose; the claim of disinterested beauty.
- Umberto Eco: semiotics, codes, interpretation, over-interpretation.
- Theodor Adorno: culture industry, standardization, administered pleasure, aesthetic resistance.

Guardrail:
- Do not flatten beauty into propaganda. Audit how beauty functions in a concrete scene.

### 7. Media-Material Axis

Question: What physical carrier, interface, protocol, infrastructure, speed, storage, and failure mode make the object possible?

Use for: AI tools, command-line interfaces, cloud storage, phones, platforms, streaming, archives, software, hardware failure, urban transport, communication systems.

Core mechanism:
- Information is never weightless. It depends on devices, protocols, energy, storage, screens, networks, interfaces, standards, and physical maintenance.
- The medium is not a neutral container; it changes perception, behavior, social organization, and possible thought forms.

Useful lenses:
- Marshall McLuhan: the medium is the message; media forms reshape society beyond their content.
- Matthew G. Kirschenbaum: formal and forensic materiality; digital traces still have physical substrates.
- Paul Virilio: speed, dromology, original accident; every technology invents its own disaster.

Guardrail:
- Do not turn infrastructure into fate. Identify which material constraint actually matters in the case.

## Dual-Track Framework

### Track A: Academic Standard Answer

Use this track to produce an academically defensible standard-answer explanation before the audit, then reduce that explanation to first-principles mechanisms:
- What is the object?
- What are the key facts, definitions, and domain contexts?
- How is it structured?
- What is the mainstream academic or technically defensible account?
- What mechanism, form, structure, or historical condition makes it work?
- What remains true if labels, schools, and authority citations are removed?
- What would a strong academic answer need to say before any critical interpretation begins?

Output style:
- Concrete, causal, layered.
- Prefer mechanisms over adjectives.
- Explain what must be true for the object to function.

### Track B: Thinker Framework / Dimensional Audit Model

Use this track to interpret symbolic, ideological, libidinal, and conceptual structures through the 4+3 model:
- What desire is being organized?
- What identity is being mirrored?
- What fantasy makes the structure tolerable?
- What signs hide the material operation?
- What narrative turns contingency into destiny?
- What aesthetic form makes power feel natural?

Output style:
- Dense, interpretive, conceptually sharp.
- Use theory to increase precision, not to decorate.
- Mark speculative readings clearly.

## Axis Priority Rule

For any substantial audit, start from the 4 main axes:

- Physical-neural
- Social discipline
- Ontological existence
- Desire-mirror

Use the 3 secondary axes when they are prominent in the object:

- Narrative-logic
- Aesthetics-rhetoric
- Media-material

If a main axis is clearly weak for the object, compress it instead of forcing a long section. If a secondary axis is decisive, promote it into the main analysis.
